# Getting started on Fiverr: zero to your first $100

The hard part at the start isn't skill, it's trust. You have none yet, so the
whole early game is about earning your first reviews cheaply and fast, then
using that proof to raise prices.

## Set up an account that converts

- **Profile photo:** a real, clear photo of your face on a plain background.
- **Username:** professional and memorable; avoid random numbers.
- **Bio:** in under ~200 words, the problem you solve, who you solve it for, and
  why you specifically. Skip the life story.
- **Skills & languages:** list them specifically and mark language fluency
  honestly.
- **Identity verification:** complete it early; it smooths your first withdrawal.

## Your first gig

**Title pattern:** `I will [specific deliverable] for [specific audience]`.
Specific beats broad every time:

- Too vague: "I will do graphic design"
- Good: "I will design a modern logo for your startup"
- Good: "I will write SEO blog posts for SaaS companies"

**Pricing with zero reviews.** Start below your eventual rate to buy momentum,
then climb as reviews accumulate:

| Your real rate | Starter price | Raise after |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| $50+ | $15-25 | ~5 orders |
| $100+ | $25-40 | ~10 orders |
| $200+ | $40-75 | ~15-20 orders |

Then step prices up roughly 10-20% every 5-10 clean orders.

**Three packages, three jobs:**
- **Basic:** the smallest real deliverable, at an entry price.
- **Standard:** what most buyers actually need; position it as the best value.
- **Premium:** the full treatment plus extras, priced to anchor.

Logo example: Basic ($25) one concept, PNG only · Standard ($50) three concepts,
vector files, two revisions · Premium ($100) five concepts, full brand kit,
unlimited revisions.

## Landing the first orders

What actually works early:

1. **Apply to buyer briefs daily:** matching requests are your fastest route to
   a first order.
2. **Price slightly under market:** just enough to win the early ones.
3. **Reply fast:** under an hour noticeably lifts conversion.
4. **Answer everything in the gig:** a thorough description pre-sells.
5. **Show samples:** even personal projects prove you can do the work.

**A proposal that gets read** references something specific from their request,
explains your approach in a line or two, includes a relevant sample, and ends
with a question so they reply. For example: *"Hi, saw you need [specific thing].
I've done similar for [type of client]; here's a sample [link]. I'd approach it
by [one line]. Quick question: [something about their project]?"*

Don't: paste generic proposals, underbid so hard you can't deliver well, promise
timelines you can't hit, or manufacture reviews (friends, purchases). Fake
reviews are a permanent-ban risk and they poison your ranking.

## A rough first-30-days arc

| Week | Aim | Focus |
|------|-----|-------|
| 1 | 1-2 gigs live | Polish profile and gig copy |
| 2 | First order | Apply to 10+ briefs a day |
| 3 | 3-5 orders | Over-deliver, ask politely for reviews |
| 4 | Raise prices ~20% | Keep what's working, cut what isn't |

## Mistakes that stall beginners

- Launching too many gigs at once instead of perfecting one or two.
- Pricing so low it attracts the worst clients.
- Promising delivery times with no buffer for revisions.
- Ignoring impressions/analytics: they tell you which gig has legs.
- Never asking for the review after a happy delivery.
