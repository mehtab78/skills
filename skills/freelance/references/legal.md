# Legal notes for AI-assisted freelancing

Not legal advice, orientation. The stakes rise fast once real clients and money
are involved, so when in doubt, get proper counsel and read each platform's
current terms.

This is the canonical cross-jurisdiction treatment; the `fiverr` and `upwork`
skills point back here instead of repeating it, and only carry their own
platform-specific deltas. If you update the EU/US/UK disclosure or copyright
rules below, that's the one place it needs to change.

## Terms-of-service risk

Every major platform treats full automation and impersonation as serious
violations:

- **Upwork:** must keep a human in the loop; no automation.
- **Fiverr:** no AI-generated fake profiles, deepfakes, or fake engagement.
- **Toptal:** no impersonation, no automated scraping.
- **Freelancer:** accounts must represent real people.

Enforcement escalates: warning → restriction → suspension → permanent ban →
potential legal action.

## Disclosure

- **EU (AI Act):** disclosure is generally mandatory when users interact with an
  AI system, when AI-generated content is published, or when deepfakes are
  involved, with steep penalties for non-compliance.
- **US:** no federal disclosure law, but the FTC treats deceptive AI claims as a
  consumer-protection violation, and some states add their own rules.
- **UK:** no dedicated AI-disclosure law; general consumer-protection and
  advertising standards still apply.

## You own the output

The liability chain runs AI provider → you (the deployer) → client → third
parties, and **you are responsible for what the AI produces**. "I didn't review
it" is not a defense, recent cases have held companies liable for their AI's
misrepresentations and for overpromising AI capabilities. Review everything before
it goes out.

## Copyright

- **US:** purely AI-generated work generally isn't copyrightable; meaningful
  human editing strengthens the claim.
- **EU:** similar, a human author is required; AI is treated as a tool.
- **UK:** more permissive toward computer-generated works, with authorship
  attributed to the person who arranged the creation.

The work-for-hire catch: if the output isn't copyrightable, there may be nothing
to actually transfer to the client. Add real human authorship.

## The fraud line

- **Fine:** AI as an acknowledged, disclosed tool, with human review.
- **Problematic:** AI doing most of the work, undisclosed.
- **Fraud:** AI impersonating a human identity, fake credentials, fabricated
  portfolio pieces or testimonials, promising human work but delivering pure AI,
  or training on client data without consent.

## Practical checklist

1. Keep a human reviewing output before delivery.
2. Default to disclosing AI assistance.
3. Document your workflow and human contribution.
4. Make sure any professional insurance covers AI-assisted work.
5. For EU clients, always disclose AI-generated components.
